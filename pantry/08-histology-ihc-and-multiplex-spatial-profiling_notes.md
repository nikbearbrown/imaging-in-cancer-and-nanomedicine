## A. Conceptual foundations

### Model-to-human gap

Research methods trade control for realism. Cell culture, mouse models, imaging endpoints, biomarkers, and trials each answer different questions.

**Common misconception:** Students expect one experiment to prove clinical value.

**Worked example:** Build a chain from in vitro mechanism to animal biodistribution to phase I safety to randomized benefit.

**Source(s):** See the chapter source bank below.

### Bias control

Good cancer research is designed to avoid self-deception: controls, randomization, prespecified endpoints, adequate power, blinding where possible, and transparent analysis.

**Common misconception:** Students see methods as bureaucratic rather than epistemic.

**Worked example:** Identify which bias a design feature prevents.

**Source(s):** See the chapter source bank below.

### Translation requires constraints

Regulatory, manufacturing, access, toxicity, and cost constraints are part of whether a discovery becomes medicine.

**Common misconception:** Students think efficacy alone makes a therapy real.

**Worked example:** Follow a candidate from preclinical success to dose finding, manufacturing, trial endpoints, approval, and access.

**Source(s):** See the chapter source bank below.

## D. Current state of the field

**Settled:**
- Mechanism and evidence must be linked. A tool, treatment, test, or platform is meaningful only if it changes a decision or measures a biologically interpretable signal.
- Cancer care and research depend on context: tumor type, stage, molecular profile, patient condition, model system, modality, and endpoint.

**Contested or emerging:**
- Biomarker-driven and image-guided approaches continue to expand, but many signals remain imperfect proxies for clinical benefit.
- Nanomedicine, liquid biopsy, quantitative imaging, theranostics, and AI-assisted interpretation are active areas; distinguish established clinical use from research promise.
- For treatment chapters, current standards change quickly and should be checked against current guidelines before publication.

**Recent developments to acknowledge:**
- Rapid expansion of molecular diagnostics, liquid biopsy, PSMA/DOTATATE theranostics, quantitative imaging, and biomarker-defined clinical trials.
- FDA oncology initiatives increasingly emphasize dose optimization, confirmatory evidence, and endpoints that demonstrate meaningful clinical benefit.
- Cancer nanotechnology increasingly integrates delivery, imaging, immune modulation, and theranostics, but translation remains constrained by characterization and reproducibility.

## E. Teaching considerations

**Where students get stuck:**
- They confuse the name of a modality or therapy with the reason it works.
- They forget the denominator: who is being tested, treated, imaged, or studied?
- They treat a positive signal as sufficient rather than asking what it predicts and what it misses.

**Analogies and framings that work:**
- Map each chapter as a chain: mechanism → measurement/tool → decision → failure mode.
- For imaging and nanomedicine, use scale ladders and dose-loss funnels.
- For medicine and research, use decision trees and endpoint tables.

**Exercises that build the target skill:**
- Mechanism-to-decision map: draw how a biological mechanism changes a clinical/research choice.
- Proxy audit: identify what a test or endpoint measures, what it does not measure, and when it misleads.
- Case triage: choose the next test, treatment frame, model, or imaging modality and defend the choice.
