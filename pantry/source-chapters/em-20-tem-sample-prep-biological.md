# Chapter 20 — TEM Sample Preparation for Biological Materials


## TL;DR

- Five days of chemistry to earn thirty minutes of imaging.
- The chapter moves through Warm-up, Application, Synthesis, Challenge.
- Read it for the main argument, the vocabulary it introduces, and the practical judgment it asks you to develop.

*Five days of chemistry to earn thirty minutes of imaging. The pipeline is the experiment.*

---

A graduate student wraps a freshly extracted one-millimeter cube of mouse cardiac tissue in dialysis tubing and submerges it in cold glutaraldehyde. The clock starts. Two hours in the cold room. Three rinses. One hour in osmium tetroxide in the fume hood — the student works carefully; the vapor is acutely toxic. Three more rinses. The graded ethanol series: 30%, 50%, 70%, 90%, 100%, 100%, 100% — each step replacing water with solvent while the tissue swells just slightly, then stabilizes. Overnight in ethanol-resin mix. A day in pure resin rotating on a wheel. Into an embedding mold. Sixty hours in the oven at 60°C. Five days after the tissue came out of the animal, the student takes the now-hard resin block to the ultramicrotome, trims the face to a trapezoid under the stereomicroscope, advances the diamond knife, and begins cutting sections seventy nanometers thick. Each section floats off the knife onto a trough of water, silver-colored in reflected light, so thin it would be invisible if not for that interference color. The student picks up sections on copper grids, stains with uranyl acetate and then lead citrate, dries the grids, and loads one into the TEM.

The first image at 50,000× shows mitochondria with crisp cristae, ribosomes as distinct dots on rough endoplasmic reticulum, and intercalated discs between cardiac myocytes. It took five days to produce thirty minutes of imaging.

That is the ratio — and the discipline — of biological TEM preparation.

---

The reason the preparation takes so long is that every step solves a physical problem, and every physical problem takes time to solve properly. To understand why the steps are ordered the way they are, start with what the TEM actually demands of a specimen.

The TEM requires three things. The specimen must survive high vacuum — no free water. It must survive the electron beam without burning, melting, or charging. And it must be thin enough that the electron beam passes through: roughly 100 nm or less for conventional TEM at 80–120 kV, thinner for higher resolution. A living cell fails all three requirements. It is mostly water. It is made of light elements — carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen — that provide essentially no electron contrast even when dried. And its smallest features are nanometers in size while the cell itself is micrometers across, meaning you cannot thin it without destroying it unless it is first embedded in something hard.

---

The first step is fixation, and it is the most critical, because every subsequent step acts on what fixation has produced.

Glutaraldehyde is a five-carbon molecule with an aldehyde group at each end. In contact with the amino groups on proteins, it forms covalent crosslinks — molecular bridges between adjacent protein chains. The crosslinks do two things simultaneously: they lock the spatial arrangement of proteins in place at the moment the fixative arrived, and they begin the process of hardening the cell's soft interior into a network that will hold its shape during the chemical violence of dehydration, embedding, and microtomy.

The word "fixation" is instructive. The cell is fixed — made chemically inert, structurally immobile — at the instant the glutaraldehyde reaches it. Whatever was happening in that cell at that moment is now preserved. Which raises an immediate concern: if you want to capture a specific cellular process, the state of the cell when the fixative arrives is the state you record. Fast processes — vesicle fusion, organelle movement, cytoskeletal dynamics — may already have changed in the seconds between the stimulus and the moment fixative penetrates deep enough to arrest everything. This is one of the fundamental limitations of chemical fixation, and it matters enormously for questions about dynamic cellular events.

The glutaraldehyde is dissolved in a buffer — typically 0.1 M cacodylate or phosphate buffer at pH 7.2–7.4. The buffer's job is to maintain the osmolarity and pH of the fixative solution close to the cell's native conditions. Get this wrong — too concentrated, wrong pH — and the cells begin to swell or shrink from osmotic stress before the fixative can lock them in place. The morphology in the final TEM image then reflects osmotic damage rather than native structure.

Fixation is followed by post-fixation with osmium tetroxide. The osmium step does something that glutaraldehyde cannot: it reacts with the carbon-carbon double bonds in unsaturated fatty acids, crosslinking the lipids in cell membranes. Membranes are what make a cell architecturally coherent — the plasma membrane, the nuclear envelope, the mitochondrial cristae, the rough and smooth ER — and without osmium fixation they would disorganize during the ethanol dehydration that follows. Osmium also has an atomic number of 76, which means that any structure enriched in osmium-bound lipid becomes electron-dense: it scatters electrons strongly and appears dark in TEM bright-field images. The osmium is simultaneously fixing and staining. The reason mitochondrial cristae are visible in biological TEM is largely that their membranes contain osmium.

The vapor from osmium tetroxide is acutely toxic. It has a particular affinity for corneal and pulmonary tissue. The concentration threshold at which it begins causing damage is low enough that by the time you can smell it, you have already been exposed to a meaningful dose. It is handled only in a fume hood, only in sealed vials opened with deliberate care, and the working rule is simple: smell nothing. If you smell something, stop immediately.

---

After osmium post-fixation, the specimen is fixed and chemically stable but still full of water. The TEM requires that it not be. Removing the water is dehydration, and the key to doing it without destroying the specimen is to do it slowly.

The standard procedure is a graded series of ethanol concentrations: 30%, 50%, 70%, 90%, 100%, 100%, 100%. At each step, some water leaves the specimen and some ethanol enters. The gradual exchange allows the specimen to equilibrate osmotically at each concentration before the next step strips away more water. Jumping directly from aqueous buffer to pure ethanol would collapse cell membranes and shrink the specimen from osmotic shock faster than the fixed molecular network could resist. The graded series minimizes the driving force for structural distortion at each step.

Ethanol extracts some lipid-soluble material from the specimen as it penetrates. This is unavoidable; all dehydration solvents do it to some degree. The degree of extraction is one of the compromises that chemical fixation demands.

After complete dehydration, the specimen sits in pure ethanol. Ethanol is not what you want it in — it is not a structural support, and it will evaporate. What you want is for the ethanol to be replaced by something that will polymerize into a hard solid around the specimen: resin.

---

Resin infiltration is the slowest part of the conventional protocol because the resin molecules are large and viscous and must penetrate every part of the specimen before polymerization. The process is graded just like dehydration: the ethanol is progressively replaced by resin through a series of ethanol-resin mixtures, rotating on a wheel for two to four hours at each concentration, ending in pure resin for multiple changes. Then the specimen, now thoroughly infiltrated, is transferred to an embedding mold and placed in an oven at 60°C for two to three days. The resin polymerizes around it, converting the soft tissue into a solid block from which sections can be cut.

The resin serves as a mechanical matrix. When the ultramicrotome knife passes through the block, the resin holds the cellular structure in place, preventing the membrane networks and organelles from flexing and tearing as the knife passes. Without the resin matrix, cutting a 70-nm section from tissue would be like trying to slice a 70-nm shaving off a loosely packed sponge — the material would simply compress and tear rather than cut cleanly.

---

Cutting the sections is the most technically demanding step, and the step where specimen quality can most easily be undone by bad technique.

The ultramicrotome is a precision instrument whose central mechanism is simple: a specimen block is advanced toward a knife in small, controlled increments — typically 60–90 nm per stroke — and the knife trims a section from the block face on each pass. The sections float free onto the surface of water in a trough mounted beside the knife, where they can be picked up onto copper grids.

The knife must be sharp. Glass knives are freshly broken from plate glass — a clean fracture produces an edge sharp enough for soft biological materials but which dulls quickly. Diamond knives are expensive but last for many specimens and handle harder resin-embedded materials better. Either knife sits in its trough of water. The surface tension of the water supports the sections as they float free.

The first cuts through a freshly trimmed block face are thick — one to two micrometers — to confirm that the region of interest is actually at the cutting face. These thick sections go onto glass slides and are stained with toluidine blue for light microscopy. If the light-microscope image shows the right cell types in the right configuration, the operator begins thinning: reducing the advance per stroke until the sections cut in the 60–90 nm range, identifiable by their silver interference color in reflected light.

Section thickness is read by color. This is one of the stranger practical skills in electron microscopy: the sections are so thin that they diffract visible light and produce interference colors the way a soap film does, with the color corresponding to thickness. Silver sections are roughly 50–70 nm — the standard range for conventional TEM. Gold sections are 70–90 nm, still usable but slightly thick. Gray sections are below 50 nm, suitable for HRTEM when available. Purple sections are above 100 nm, thick enough to produce good low-magnification contrast but too thick for high-resolution work.

| Color | Approximate thickness | TEM use |
|---|---|---|
| **Gray** | < 50 nm | HRTEM, high-resolution work; thin enough that beam transmission is excellent |
| **Silver** | 50–70 nm | Standard TEM imaging — the workhorse range |
| **Gold** | 70–90 nm | Usable but thicker; resolution begins to suffer; staining contrast may help |
| **Purple** | > 100 nm | Low-magnification survey only; loss of resolution from chromatic spread |

*Useful chairside reference during microtomy sessions to assess sections without a thickness gauge.*

Ultramicrotomy produces a characteristic set of artifacts when something goes wrong. Chatter — parallel ridges crossing the section perpendicular to the cutting direction — comes from vibration in the knife or specimen arm, often from a dull knife or from loose components. Knife marks are parallel scratches parallel to the cutting direction, from a defect in the knife edge. Compression along the cutting direction causes sections to be shorter than the corresponding block dimension; the compression ratio depends on resin hardness and knife angle and is partially relieved by floating the sections on a water surface with slight warming. Wrinkles form when sections pick up unevenly from the water surface, especially when humidity is low or the grid is not positioned properly.

---

The sections on their grids have excellent structural preservation and excellent dimensional fidelity relative to the original specimen, but they have almost no inherent electron contrast. The cell is made of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen — all low-Z elements that scatter electrons only weakly. The osmium bound to membranes provides some contrast, but ribosomes, chromatin, and many cytoplasmic structures are still nearly invisible without additional staining.

Heavy-metal stains solve this. The standard two-step protocol uses uranyl acetate followed by lead citrate. Uranyl acetate — a uranium salt — binds electrostatically to nucleic acids and proteins, increasing their electron density. Lead citrate then binds to sites that have been modified by uranyl, amplifying the contrast further and staining membranes, ribosomes, glycogen, and other structures. The combined effect is dramatic: structures that were invisible become clearly visible, and the contrast between different cellular components allows their identification.

Both reagents are hazardous. Uranyl acetate contains uranium-238, which is mildly radioactive and chemically toxic. It is photosensitive — ultraviolet light reduces uranyl to a less soluble form that precipitates out of solution and deposits on the sections as granular "snow." Staining is done in subdued light and solutions are filtered. Lead citrate is toxic, and lead carbonate — formed when lead ions encounter atmospheric CO₂ — precipitates as a contamination on the grid. Lead staining is done in a closed container with sodium hydroxide pellets to absorb CO₂ from the air. Both reagents are disposed of as hazardous waste.

After staining and drying, the grid is ready. Five days of work, held on a copper mesh 3 mm in diameter.

---

All of that — the fixation, osmication, dehydration, embedding, microtomy, staining — applies when chemical fixation is adequate. Sometimes it is not.

The fundamental limit of chemical fixation is time. Glutaraldehyde diffuses through tissue at a finite rate — a few hundred micrometers per minute — and cells continue living, organelles continue moving, and biochemical reactions continue running until the fixative reaches them. For most structural studies, the structural snapshot captured by aldehyde fixation is adequate: the mitochondria are where the mitochondria were, the membranes are continuous, the ribosomes are where they were. But if the question concerns a dynamic process — vesicle fusion events that take milliseconds, cytoskeletal rearrangements that happen in seconds, signaling events triggered by a stimulus applied just before fixation — then chemical fixation is too slow and too nonuniform to capture the true state.

High-pressure freezing is the physical alternative. The specimen is inserted into a device that applies pressures exceeding 2,000 bar while simultaneously cooling the specimen at rates above 10,000°C per second. Under these conditions, water vitrifies — solidifies into amorphous glass rather than crystalline ice — simultaneously throughout the specimen, instantly halting all biological processes at the same moment. No diffusion lag. No uneven fixation. The cellular state at the instant of freezing is preserved in three dimensions.

The constraint is specimen size: effective vitrification requires the freezing front to reach every part of the specimen before any ice crystals form, which limits the thickness to roughly 200 micrometers. Larger specimens require cryo-protectants that further complicate interpretation. But for thin tissue pieces, cell suspensions, or monolayer cultures, high-pressure freezing produces structural preservation that chemical fixation cannot match.

After high-pressure freezing, the water is removed by freeze substitution: the vitrified specimen is placed in a cold solvent — typically acetone or methanol at around -90°C — which dissolves the water slowly while the specimen remains solid. Fixatives dissolved in the substitution solvent then chemically fix the now-dehydrated tissue during the slow warm-up to room temperature. The result is a specimen that has been fixed without the artifacts of aqueous chemical fixation, ready for resin embedding and conventional ultramicrotomy.

---

The biological TEM preparation pipeline is, in a sense, the most honest part of the whole imaging process — and the most dishonest. Honest, because every step is documented and the artifacts of every step are known and named. Dishonest, because the published TEM image looks like a window into the living cell, when it is actually a record of how the cell responded to five days of chemical and mechanical intervention.

The skill in biological TEM preparation is not just executing the protocol correctly. It is knowing which alterations each step introduces, recognizing them in the image when they appear, and distinguishing them from the genuine biology the experiment was designed to reveal. The student who completes the five-day pipeline and produces a grid full of beautiful sections has demonstrated technical competence. The microscopist who can look at those sections and say "this dilation of the ER lumen is a dehydration artifact, but this mitochondrial morphology is real" has demonstrated scientific judgment.

Both are necessary. Neither, alone, is sufficient.

---

**What would change my mind:** evidence that conventional aldehyde fixation can match high-pressure freezing for dynamic cellular events on the millisecond timescale. The structural biology literature consistently shows that HPF/freeze substitution preserves rapid processes that chemical fixation halts non-uniformly. For static structural questions in robust specimens, the comparison is closer — but for fast dynamics, the physical instantaneity of HPF has no chemical equivalent.

**Still puzzling:** the choice between epoxy resins (Epon, Spurr's) and acrylic resins (LR White, Lowicryl) for routine embeddings is largely tradition in most labs. Systematic comparisons on cutting quality, antigenicity preservation, and structural fidelity exist but are not routinely consulted. A specimen embedded in Epon at one institution and LR White at another will look subtly different in the TEM for reasons unrelated to biology — and few published methods sections acknowledge this.
