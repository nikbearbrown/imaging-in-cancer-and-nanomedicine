# Chapter 10 — Advanced SEM: FIB-SEM, Dual Beam Systems, and Variable Pressure SEM


## TL;DR

- This chapter gives a working overview of Advanced SEM: FIB-SEM, Dual Beam Systems, and Variable Pressure SEM, focusing on the ideas a reader needs before moving to the next chapter.
- The electron beam shows you where to look.
- Read it for the main argument, the vocabulary it introduces, and the practical judgment it asks you to develop.

*The electron beam shows you where to look. The ion beam cuts you a door.*

---

A semiconductor failure-analysis lab has a chip that stopped working after eighteen months of operation. The surface looks clean under the optical microscope. The SEM finds nothing wrong at the surface either. The failure is somewhere inside, buried under a passivation oxide, probably a void or a break in the metallization a few layers down. The chip is the only one. You cannot grind it down to the failure plane; you might grind through it. You cannot slice it conventionally without losing site specificity — the failure is at one specific transistor out of millions, and random mechanical cross-sectioning is almost certainly going to miss it.

What the technician does instead is open the dual-beam FIB-SEM, navigate to the suspect transistor by SEM imaging, deposit a thin platinum protective strip over the region of interest to shield it from what comes next, and then use the second column — the ion column — to mill a clean rectangular trench alongside the transistor. The ion beam removes material the way sandblasting removes paint: not by melting or burning, but by transferring momentum to surface atoms until they fly off. The trench is 5 μm wide and 10 μm deep and takes about twenty minutes to mill. The electron column, watching from its own 52° angle, images the cross-section face as it forms. When the trench is deep enough, the failure is visible: a small void in a tungsten interconnect three metal layers below the surface, with electromigration whiskers radiating outward. Root-cause analysis complete without destroying the chip, without a day of mechanical specimen preparation, without any uncertainty about whether the prepared face intercepted the right feature.

That is what a dual-beam FIB-SEM does. The electron beam shows you where to look; the ion beam cuts you a door.

---

The ion column works by physics that electrons simply cannot replicate. A gallium ion accelerated through 30 kilovolts has a mass 130,000 times that of an electron. At the same kinetic energy, the gallium ion is moving far more slowly — it has far more momentum per unit velocity, but the slow speed means it deposits that momentum shallowly, within the top few nanometers of the specimen surface. When a gallium ion arrives at a copper atom sitting in a metal lattice, the collision cascade it initiates — a series of elastic billiard-ball collisions among neighboring atoms — can knock surface atoms out of the solid entirely. That process is called **sputtering**, and the number of atoms ejected per incident ion is the **sputter yield**.

Electrons cannot sputter. They are too light. An electron arriving at the specimen surface at 30 keV is moving at a substantial fraction of the speed of light and passes straight through the first few atomic layers before depositing most of its energy, and when it does interact it does so through electromagnetic effects — inner-shell ionization, X-ray production — not by knocking lattice atoms out of position. The mass difference is the whole story: momentum transferred in a collision scales with the masses involved, and an electron simply does not have enough mass to transfer enough momentum to eject a copper atom.

Sputter yield depends on several things. It varies strongly with material: zinc at 30 keV gallium gives a yield of about 13 atoms per ion; copper about 7; aluminum about 3.5; silicon about 2. Higher-Z metals, loosely speaking, tend to yield more. It also varies with the angle at which the ion arrives at the surface. For perpendicular incidence, the collision cascade runs deep into the specimen and many of the knocked-out atoms find their way back to rest inside the material rather than escaping. As incidence angle increases toward grazing — tilting the specimen so that the ion beam arrives nearly parallel to the surface — the cascade concentrates nearer the surface, and a higher fraction of affected atoms can escape. Maximum sputter yield typically occurs somewhere between 60° and 80° from the surface normal. Beyond that, grazing incidence starts to cause reflection rather than implantation, and yield drops back down. The ion-beam operator learning to mill efficiently is really learning to navigate these angular dependencies on the fly.

The ion source is itself a marvel of engineering that is easy to take for granted. A liquid-metal ion source works by heating gallium — which melts at the inconveniently useful temperature of 30°C, just above room temperature — in a small reservoir and allowing it to wet a sharp tungsten needle. An applied electric field pulls the molten gallium into a shape at the needle's tip called a **Taylor cone**, named for Geoffrey Taylor who described the mathematics of electrostatic cone formation in conducting liquids. The cone's apex has a radius of curvature on the order of 2 nanometers — small enough that the local electric field reaches values where field ionization of gallium atoms begins. Ions extracted from that two-nanometer apex are the beam. The first practical liquid-metal ion source was demonstrated in 1975; modern versions are commercially mass-produced components that sit at the top of every FIB column in every dual-beam instrument in every failure-analysis lab in the semiconductor industry.

---

The two columns of a dual-beam instrument are mounted at a fixed angle to each other — typically 52° in FEI and Thermo Fisher instruments, which dominate the market — and their focal points converge at a single location called the **eucentric coincidence point**. The geometry matters operationally: the specimen has to sit at this exact point for the electron beam and the ion beam to both be imaging and milling the same location simultaneously. Moving the stage translates the specimen in the chamber; tilting the stage changes which surface faces each column. The standard operating posture tilts the specimen to 52° from horizontal so that the ion beam arrives perpendicular to the surface of interest while the electron beam, angled 52° away, images from a high take-off angle. It is an awkward geometry — the electron-beam images look foreshortened because you are imaging a tilted surface — but it is the arrangement that lets both columns work on the same feature at the same time.

The practical consequence of working at the eucentric point is that milling and imaging are simultaneous. You watch the cross-section form on the screen, in real time, through the electron column, while the ion column excavates it. This is not a minor convenience; it is what makes site-specific failure analysis practical. If the milling and imaging were sequential — mill for a while, then tilt to image, then tilt back to mill — you would rapidly lose track of where you were in the specimen and how deep you had gone.

The FIB can also deposit material, not just remove it. If a gas precursor is injected near the milling site — typically an organometallic compound like platinum or tungsten carbonyl — the ion beam (or the electron beam, which works more gently) decomposes the gas, depositing the metal and leaving the volatile components to pump away. This is how the protective platinum strip is deposited before milling a critical cross-section: a thin platinum layer is laid down directly on the region of interest, shielding the top surface from the oblique ion beam that would otherwise damage it. The same technique can deposit conductive bridges to repair a broken interconnect — a capability called circuit edit that the semiconductor industry uses to prototype chip modifications without resynthesizing silicon.

For the most demanding application — preparing a sample thin enough for TEM imaging — the FIB is used to mill a lamella: a thin slab of material, typically under 100 nanometers thick, lifted out of the bulk specimen by a micromanipulator and transferred to a TEM grid. The lamella can come from any chosen location on any surface. This procedure has transformed TEM specimen preparation for inorganic materials and devices; where conventional cross-sectioning was a day-long grinding and polishing exercise with unpredictable outcomes, FIB lamella preparation is a two-to-four-hour process with chosen and reproducible outcomes. Chapter 22 covers the details.

---

The price of ion-beam milling is ion-beam damage. It is unavoidable and it matters.

Every milled surface has a thin damaged layer where the collision cascades left behind crystal-lattice defects, amorphized the material, and implanted gallium. The damage layer from a 30 keV gallium beam is typically 10 to 30 nanometers thick. For a cross-section viewed at micrometer-scale resolution by SEM, this damage layer is invisible — the feature being examined is far larger than the damaged zone. For a TEM lamella being imaged at atomic resolution, the damage layer is a substantial fraction of the total lamella thickness, and it appears in the TEM image as an amorphous surface band that obscures the crystalline structure underneath. High-resolution TEM of FIB lamellae is not directly possible from the as-milled surface; the lamella must be cleaned up by a lower-energy ion polishing step, typically at 2–5 keV, that removes the damaged zone without penetrating deeply enough to add a new one.

Several other artifacts appear with enough regularity that they have names. **Curtaining** — parallel vertical striations running across the cross-section face — appears when the milled surface has alternating regions of high and low sputter yield, such as alternating hard and soft layers or buried voids. The ion beam mills faster through the soft regions, leaving ridges behind the hard ones. The mitigation is to deposit a thick protective platinum layer and to use careful scan strategies, but curtaining in layered specimens is never fully eliminated. **Redeposition** occurs when sputtered atoms travel a short distance and land on an adjacent surface rather than escaping the chamber; in deep narrow features it can build up walls and fill structures that should be empty. **Ion implantation** leaves residual gallium in the first few nanometers of every milled surface, which alters local conductivity and chemistry — important for electrical characterization and sometimes detectable in EDS as a spurious gallium signal from surfaces that should contain none.

An operator doing quantitative work near a FIB-cut surface has to hold all of this in mind. The image of the cross-section is not the cross-section; it is the cross-section plus a damage layer plus redeposited material at some surfaces. The artifacts are known, they are manageable, and a competent operator accounts for them. But they are not zero.

---

The other advanced mode in this chapter has different physics and solves a different problem.

A variable-pressure SEM operates with the specimen chamber at pressures between 1 and 2,500 pascals — many orders of magnitude above the conventional $10^{-5}$ Pa of a standard SEM column. The gas is typically water vapor, occasionally nitrogen or argon. The point is to introduce enough gas into the chamber to neutralize the surface charge that accumulates on insulating or poorly-conducting specimens.

In a conventional SEM, an insulating specimen accumulates beam-injected charge faster than it can dissipate it. The resulting charge distorts the image — dark patches, bright haloes, streaking, wild swings in signal when the beam scans over a charged region. The standard solution is conductive coating: sputter-deposit a thin layer of gold, palladium, or carbon onto the surface to give the charge a conductive path off the specimen. This works but it irreversibly modifies the surface, potentially obscures fine surface features under the coating, and makes the specimen unsuitable for subsequent chemical analysis through EDS because the coating's signal overlaps with the specimen's.

VP-SEM solves it differently. At 200 Pa of water vapor, gas molecules are ionized by the beam and by the backscattered electrons returning from the specimen. The resulting positive ions drift to negatively charged regions of the surface and neutralize them. No coating needed. The specimen is imaged as-is.

The catch is that the same gas that neutralizes the charge also scatters the primary beam. At 200 Pa with a 10 mm path length and 20 keV electrons, roughly 80% of the beam electrons scatter off gas molecules before reaching the specimen, spreading into a broad "skirt" that illuminates a wide area rather than the focused probe. Only about 20% of the beam electrons arrive at the specimen as a focused probe. This sounds catastrophic, but it is not: the 20% that arrive focused still form a focused image with the same resolution as conventional SEM. The other 80% add a diffuse background to the signal that reduces contrast and signal-to-noise but does not blur the focused component. The image is noisier than a conventional SEM image of the same specimen, but the resolution is comparable. Long pixel dwell times compensate for the reduced contrast.

The detector constraints are severe. The Everhart-Thornley detector's +10 kilovolt scintillator bias would cause a gas discharge at 200 Pa — the chamber gas would break down between the scintillator and the Faraday cage, arcing and destroying the signal. The E-T is simply unusable in VP mode. BSE detectors without bias work fine; backscattered electrons have enough energy to reach them without acceleration. For secondary-electron imaging, VP-SEM uses the **gaseous secondary-electron detector** (GSED): an electrode held at a few hundred volts — below the gas-breakdown threshold — attracts SEs from the specimen, and the SEs undergo avalanche multiplication as they collide with gas molecules on the way to the electrode. The GSED converts the chamber gas from a scattering obstacle into an amplification medium. With the right electrode geometry and pressure, the cascade gain compensates for the reduced beam intensity and gives an SE-quality image.

The specimen range that VP-SEM enables is substantial. Hydrated biological tissue can be imaged without fixation or dehydration, by maintaining the chamber at appropriate humidity — typically water vapor partial pressure close to the equilibrium vapor pressure above the specimen at the chamber temperature. A fresh, unfixed biological surface looks different from a fixed-and-dried one; sometimes the difference is the scientific question. Polymers that cannot be coated without altering surface properties. Archaeological specimens that are irreplaceable and must not be modified. Geological samples with high porosity where a conductive coating would be meaningless. Dirty industrial parts where contamination is part of the question and cleaning would remove it.

---

The discipline for choosing between these modes is the same discipline as all the technique choices before: start with the question and derive the instrument.

FIB-SEM is the right answer when the question is *what is at this specific location inside the specimen*. When location specificity matters, when the answer is buried beneath the surface, when you need a TEM lamella from a chosen site — FIB is the tool. The damage artifacts are the price.

VP-SEM is the right answer when the question requires imaging a specimen that conventional vacuum would destroy or that coating would misrepresent. When the specimen is hydrated, biological, insulating, irreplaceable, or dirty in a scientifically meaningful way — VP-SEM is the tool. The reduced detector flexibility and lower signal-to-noise are the price.

Conventional SEM remains the right answer for everything else. If the specimen will survive standard preparation, if you want the full detector inventory, if you need maximum resolution — the standard instrument at high vacuum with a conductive surface and the full detector set is faster, sharper, and simpler.

What is striking about the two advanced modes is how completely they are consequences of specific physical constraints. VP-SEM exists because charge on insulating surfaces defeats the E-T detector — and the solution (gas ionization) uses the same physics as the problem. FIB-SEM exists because mass determines what a beam can do to a surface — and the solution (heavy ions) exploits the mass differential directly. Neither mode is an arbitrary engineering choice. Both are what the physics required.

**What would change my mind:** evidence that ion-beam-induced damage in FIB cross-sections is universally negligible for atomic-resolution imaging without cleanup. Current evidence consistently shows damage layers in the 10–30 nm range from 30 keV gallium beams. Lower-energy plasma-FIB instruments using xenon at 5 keV reduce this substantially, but the physics of sputtering guarantees some damage at any energy where milling occurs.

**Still puzzling:** why the choice between FIB-SEM and conventional mechanical cross-section preparation for routine TEM lamella work is almost never formalized in laboratory protocols. FIB dominates in semiconductor labs because speed and site-specificity are paramount. Conventional polishing dominates in some materials-science labs because bulk lamellae avoid the damage layer entirely. Most labs default to one or the other by inertia rather than by criterion. A simple decision tree — based on whether site specificity matters and whether the damage layer is tolerable — would serve new students better than the inherited default.
