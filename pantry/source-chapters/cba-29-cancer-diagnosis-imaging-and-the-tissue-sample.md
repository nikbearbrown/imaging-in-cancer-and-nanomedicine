# Chapter 29 — Cancer Diagnosis: Imaging and the Tissue Sample


## TL;DR

- This chapter gives a working overview of Cancer Diagnosis: Imaging and the Tissue Sample, focusing on the ideas a reader needs before moving to the next chapter.
- The chapter moves through The diagnostic workup framework, Computed tomography, Magnetic resonance imaging, Ultrasound, and related ideas.
- Read it for the main argument, the vocabulary it introduces, and the practical judgment it asks you to develop.

A cancer diagnosis is not a name. It is a chain of evidence that ends with a pathologist looking at cells.

Hold the framing. When a doctor says "the patient has cancer," what has actually happened is a sequence: a clinical suspicion based on symptoms or a screening test, an imaging study that identified an abnormality, a biopsy that obtained tissue, a pathologist examining the tissue under a microscope and rendering a diagnosis. Each step is a separate diagnostic technology with its own limitations, its own false positive and false negative rates, and its own evolution over time. The final diagnosis is the integration of all these layers.

The integration matters because cancer is heterogeneous and the diagnostic process is iterative. Imaging may suggest cancer but cannot definitively prove it. Biopsy provides tissue but the tissue may not be representative. Pathology renders a diagnosis but the diagnosis may change as additional information arrives (molecular profiling, response to therapy, new biopsies of progressing disease). Modern oncology is built on diagnostic infrastructure that integrates these technologies in increasingly sophisticated ways.

This chapter — the first of two on cancer detection and diagnosis — covers the imaging modalities used to identify and characterize cancers, the biopsy techniques that obtain tissue, and the pathology approaches that interpret what is in that tissue. The next — 18B — covers molecular diagnostics (the genetic and biomarker profiling that increasingly drives treatment decisions), staging systems, and emerging diagnostic technologies including liquid biopsy.

Hold the question: *how do we go from a clinical suspicion to a confirmed cancer diagnosis with enough specificity to guide treatment?*

## The diagnostic workup framework

When cancer is suspected, the workup typically follows a sequence.

*Clinical history and physical examination*. Symptoms (weight loss, fatigue, pain, bleeding, mass), risk factors (smoking, family history, age, prior cancers), examination findings (palpable mass, lymphadenopathy, skin changes, organ enlargement). The clinical assessment generates a *pretest probability* of cancer that shapes the subsequent workup.

*Laboratory studies*. Blood counts, chemistry panels, tumor markers (when applicable). Most blood-based tumor markers are not specific enough for diagnosis but can be useful for monitoring known cancers (PSA in prostate cancer, CEA in colorectal cancer, CA-125 in ovarian cancer, AFP in hepatocellular carcinoma).

*Imaging*. Anatomical imaging (CT, MRI, ultrasound) identifies masses and characterizes their location, size, and relationship to surrounding structures. Functional imaging (PET) assesses metabolic activity. The findings determine the next steps and inform staging.

*Tissue sampling*. Biopsy or surgical specimen for pathology. Cancer diagnosis ultimately requires tissue or cell examination. Without pathology, "cancer" is a clinical hypothesis, not a diagnosis.

*Pathology and molecular workup*. Histology (the appearance of cells and tissue architecture), immunohistochemistry (specific protein markers), and increasingly molecular profiling (genetic mutations, expression patterns, biomarker testing) characterize the tumor and inform treatment.

*Staging studies*. Once cancer is confirmed, additional imaging assesses the extent of disease — local invasion, regional lymph nodes, distant metastases.

The workup is staged. Each step builds on the previous one. Negative findings can short-circuit the workup; positive findings drive deeper investigation. The sequence aims to be efficient — getting to a diagnosis with the minimum invasive and expensive testing — while being comprehensive enough to inform definitive treatment.

## Computed tomography

CT scanning is the workhorse of cancer imaging. The technology uses X-rays acquired from many angles around the patient, computationally reconstructed into cross-sectional images of internal anatomy. Modern multi-detector CT scanners produce thin-slice images with sub-millimeter resolution.

CT excels at:
- Visualizing soft tissue masses and organ anatomy.
- Detecting metastases to lung, liver, lymph nodes, bone, and other sites.
- Guiding biopsy procedures.
- Treatment planning (radiation therapy uses CT for target volume delineation).
- Monitoring response to therapy.

CT limitations:
- Soft tissue contrast is inferior to MRI for some applications (brain, spinal cord, soft tissue tumors).
- Radiation exposure (typical body CT delivers 8-15 mSv, compared to 0.1 mSv for a chest X-ray and 2-3 mSv annual background radiation).
- Iodinated contrast agents (used to enhance vascular structures and tumor visibility) carry small but real risks of allergic reactions and contrast-induced nephropathy.

Specific cancer applications:
- *Chest CT* is the standard for evaluating lung cancer, mediastinal disease, and pulmonary metastases.
- *Abdominal CT* (typically with contrast) evaluates liver, pancreatic, kidney, adrenal, and other abdominal cancers.
- *Pelvic CT* evaluates pelvic cancers and pelvic lymph nodes.
- *Whole-body CT* in metastatic workup.
- *CT-guided biopsy* allows precise targeting of small or deep lesions.

CT is also used in *quantitative imaging* to measure tumor response. Response criteria (RECIST — Response Evaluation Criteria In Solid Tumors) define how to measure tumor size on serial CT scans to assess treatment response: complete response (CR), partial response (PR), stable disease (SD), progressive disease (PD). The standardized framework allows comparison across clinical trials.

## Magnetic resonance imaging

MRI uses strong magnetic fields and radiofrequency pulses to image tissue. Different MRI sequences emphasize different tissue properties (T1-weighted, T2-weighted, FLAIR, diffusion-weighted, perfusion imaging). MRI is particularly valuable for:

- *Central nervous system cancer*. Brain and spinal cord imaging is dramatically better with MRI than CT.
- *Soft tissue tumors*. MRI provides superior contrast for muscle, fascia, soft tissue masses.
- *Pelvic cancers*. Cervical, uterine, prostate, rectal, and bladder cancer staging benefits from MRI.
- *Breast cancer*. Breast MRI is the most sensitive modality for breast cancer detection, used in screening high-risk women and in evaluating ambiguous mammographic findings.
- *Liver lesion characterization*. MRI with hepatocyte-specific contrast agents (gadoxetate) provides excellent liver imaging.
- *Cartilage and bone marrow*. MRI evaluates bone marrow involvement in lymphoma and other cancers.

MRI limitations:
- Longer acquisition times than CT (typically 30-60 minutes for a comprehensive exam, with patient cooperation required).
- Claustrophobia can be a problem.
- Implanted devices (some pacemakers, cochlear implants, certain metal foreign bodies) are contraindications.
- Gadolinium contrast agents have a rare association with nephrogenic systemic fibrosis in renal failure patients.

The choice between CT and MRI depends on the clinical question. Many workups use both for different aspects of the same case.

## Ultrasound

Ultrasound uses high-frequency sound waves to image tissue. The technology is portable, real-time, and uses no ionizing radiation. Different probe frequencies allow shallow (high-frequency, high-resolution) or deeper (lower-frequency, less resolution) imaging.

Cancer applications:
- *Thyroid, parathyroid, salivary glands*. Superficial structures imaged well.
- *Breast*. Differentiating solid from cystic lesions, evaluating mammographic findings, guiding biopsy.
- *Abdominal organs*. Liver, kidneys, biliary tree, especially in pregnant patients or for follow-up.
- *Gynecologic imaging*. Endometrial and ovarian assessment.
- *Lymph node assessment*. Especially neck and axillary nodes.
- *Image-guided procedures*. Real-time ultrasound guidance for biopsies, fluid drainage, and ablation.
- *Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS)*. A specialized probe at the end of an endoscope provides high-resolution imaging of GI tract layers, pancreas, biliary tree, and adjacent lymph nodes. EUS is the most sensitive modality for pancreatic mass detection and pancreatic cancer staging.

Ultrasound limitations include operator dependence (the diagnostic quality varies with operator skill), poor penetration through gas (limits utility for bowel, lung), and limited spatial resolution for deeper structures.

## Positron emission tomography

PET scanning uses radiolabeled tracers that accumulate in metabolically active tissue. The most widely used tracer is *fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG)*, a glucose analog that is taken up by cells through normal glucose transporters and trapped intracellularly after phosphorylation. Cancer cells, with their elevated glucose consumption (Chapter 11A), take up more FDG than normal tissue.

PET imaging shows the distribution of FDG uptake in the body. Combined with CT (PET-CT) or MRI (PET-MRI), the images show both the functional (PET) and anatomical (CT/MRI) information.

Cancer applications:
- *Staging*. Detecting metastases at sites that may have been missed on conventional imaging.
- *Lymph node assessment*. PET-positive nodes that look anatomically normal may harbor disease.
- *Treatment response monitoring*. FDG uptake decreases with effective treatment, often before tumor size changes are visible on CT.
- *Recurrence detection*. Distinguishing post-treatment changes from residual or recurrent disease.
- *Specific cancers*. Lymphoma (FDG-avid), head and neck cancer, lung cancer, colorectal cancer, melanoma, esophageal cancer, and others.

PET limitations:
- *False positives*. Inflammation, infection, brown fat, recent surgical sites, and granulomatous diseases all increase FDG uptake.
- *False negatives*. Some cancers don't take up FDG well (well-differentiated thyroid cancer, prostate cancer, some indolent tumors, mucinous tumors).
- *Cost and access*. PET-CT scans are expensive (typically $3,000-5,000) and require cyclotron-produced isotopes.
- *Radiation exposure*. Combined PET-CT delivers about 25 mSv (substantial).

Specialized PET tracers have been developed for specific applications:
- *PSMA PET* for prostate cancer (Ga-68 PSMA or F-18 PSMA tracers). Highly sensitive for prostate cancer detection and staging. FDA-approved for metastatic prostate cancer evaluation.
- *DOTATATE PET* (Ga-68) for neuroendocrine tumors (somatostatin receptor imaging).
- *Choline PET* (C-11 or F-18) for prostate cancer.
- *Fluciclovine PET* (F-18) for prostate cancer recurrence.
- *Amino acid PET tracers* (methionine, FET, FDOPA) for brain tumors.

The PET tracer field is expanding rapidly, with specialized tracers for individual molecular targets enabling increasingly precise imaging. The development is parallel to the targeted therapy revolution — molecular targets that drive cancer biology are also imaging targets.

## Other imaging modalities

*Plain radiography (X-ray)*. Limited utility in cancer diagnosis but useful for evaluating bone lesions, chest masses, and certain screening contexts (mammography is essentially a specialized form of plain radiography).

*Bone scintigraphy* (bone scan). Technetium-99m labeled phosphonate accumulates in areas of bone formation, useful for detecting bone metastases. Less specific than PET but widely available and inexpensive.

*Single photon emission computed tomography (SPECT)*. Similar to PET but uses different isotopes and provides somewhat lower resolution. Specific applications include cardiac imaging, brain imaging, and some tumor imaging contexts.

*Molecular imaging with specific tracers*. Beyond PSMA and DOTATATE PET, specific imaging targets include HER2 (for breast cancer staging), CXCR4 (for some hematological cancers), and various other molecular markers.

*Optical imaging* using fluorescent probes, photoacoustic imaging, and other technologies are emerging for intraoperative and intra-procedural use.

*Artificial intelligence in imaging*. AI tools for cancer detection on mammography, lung nodule detection on CT, and other applications are increasingly entering clinical practice. The technology aims to augment radiologist interpretation, reduce variability, and improve detection sensitivity.

## Biopsy: getting the tissue

A radiographic abnormality, however suggestive, is not a cancer diagnosis. Tissue is required. Biopsy techniques range from minimally invasive to surgical.

*Fine-needle aspiration (FNA)*. A small-gauge needle (22-25 gauge) is inserted into the suspected lesion and cells are aspirated. The cytological smear is examined for malignant cells. FNA is rapid, minimally invasive, and useful for:
- Thyroid nodules (often the first diagnostic step).
- Salivary gland masses.
- Lymph nodes.
- Some breast masses.
- EUS-guided FNA of pancreatic and other deep masses.

FNA limitations: provides cells but not tissue architecture, may be insufficient for definitive diagnosis or molecular testing, has higher rates of inconclusive results than core biopsy.

*Core needle biopsy*. A larger-gauge needle (14-18 gauge) obtains a small cylinder of tissue. Core biopsy preserves tissue architecture, allowing better histological evaluation and providing sufficient material for immunohistochemistry and molecular testing. Used for:
- Breast masses (image-guided).
- Prostate biopsies (typically transrectal ultrasound-guided, 12-core protocols).
- Liver, kidney, lung, and other organ biopsies (typically CT- or ultrasound-guided).
- Soft tissue and bone biopsies.

*Image-guided percutaneous biopsy*. Using CT, ultrasound, or MRI to target the lesion precisely. Standard approach for most deep biopsies in modern practice.

*Endoscopic biopsy*. Through GI endoscopy, bronchoscopy, cystoscopy, hysteroscopy, or other endoscopic approaches. Allows direct visualization and biopsy of lesions in accessible body cavities.

*Excisional biopsy*. The entire lesion is removed for examination. Standard for skin lesions, suspicious moles, accessible lymph nodes, and some breast lesions. Provides both diagnosis and removal in one procedure.

*Surgical biopsy*. Open surgical or laparoscopic biopsy when other approaches are insufficient. Less common in modern practice as image-guided approaches have improved.

The biopsy approach is chosen based on the location of the lesion, the suspected diagnosis, the planned management, and the patient's overall condition. A biopsy that obtains insufficient or non-diagnostic material may need to be repeated.

The *liquid biopsy* concept (discussed more in 18B) involves blood-based analysis instead of tissue biopsy. The blood-based approach has different advantages and limitations and is increasingly used in specific contexts.

## Histology: looking at the tissue

The pathologist examines biopsy material to render a diagnosis. The process involves several steps:

*Gross examination*. The pathologist describes the tissue (size, color, consistency, distinguishing features). For surgical specimens, gross examination is more elaborate, identifying tumor location, dimensions, and surgical margins.

*Fixation*. Tissue is preserved in formalin or other fixatives to prevent autolysis and allow microscopic examination. Formalin fixation followed by paraffin embedding (FFPE) is the standard.

*Sectioning*. Thin sections (typically 3-5 micrometers) are cut from the paraffin block and placed on glass slides.

*Staining*. Routine staining is hematoxylin and eosin (H&E). Hematoxylin stains nuclei blue; eosin stains cytoplasm and extracellular material pink. The combination provides good contrast for tissue architecture and cell morphology.

*Microscopic examination*. The pathologist examines the slides at various magnifications. The diagnosis is based on:
- *Cell morphology*. Size, shape, nuclear features (size, shape, hyperchromasia, nucleoli), cytoplasmic features.
- *Tissue architecture*. How the cells are organized, presence of glands, sheets, nests, or other patterns.
- *Stromal context*. The presence of fibrosis, inflammation, necrosis, vascular invasion.
- *Margins (for surgical specimens)*. Whether tumor extends to the edge of the resection.

The pathology report describes the findings and renders a diagnosis. Standard elements include:
- *Specimen type and source*. What was sampled and how.
- *Tumor type*. The histological classification (adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, etc.).
- *Tumor differentiation/grade*. How much the tumor resembles its normal tissue counterpart (well, moderately, poorly differentiated).
- *Tumor dimensions and location*.
- *Margins*. Distance from tumor to resection edge.
- *Lymphovascular invasion*. Presence of tumor cells in vessels.
- *Perineural invasion*. Tumor cells associated with nerves.
- *Lymph nodes (if included)*. Number examined, number positive.
- *Specific findings* (necrosis, mitotic count, others depending on tumor type).

The report is the foundation document for treatment decisions. It is increasingly augmented by additional studies (immunohistochemistry, molecular profiling) that further characterize the tumor.

## Immunohistochemistry

For many cancers, the H&E appearance alone is insufficient to render a specific diagnosis. *Immunohistochemistry (IHC)* uses antibodies against specific proteins to visualize their presence and distribution in tissue. The antibody is detected with a colored marker, producing brown (or other colored) staining where the protein is expressed.

IHC has multiple uses:
- *Lineage identification*. Different tumor types express different markers. Carcinomas express cytokeratins (CK7, CK20, others); lymphomas express CD45 and lineage-specific lymphoid markers (CD19, CD20 for B cells; CD3 for T cells); sarcomas express vimentin and lineage-specific markers (desmin for muscle, S100 for nerve/melanoma).
- *Differential diagnosis*. Distinguishing between two histologically similar tumors. For instance, distinguishing lung adenocarcinoma (TTF-1, napsin A positive) from metastatic adenocarcinoma from another site.
- *Origin determination*. For cancers of unknown primary, IHC panels suggest the likely tissue of origin.
- *Hormone receptor status*. ER (estrogen receptor) and PR (progesterone receptor) in breast cancer guide hormonal therapy decisions. AR (androgen receptor) in some prostate cancer contexts.
- *Specific therapeutic targets*. HER2 status in breast and gastric cancer guides HER2-targeted therapy. PD-L1 expression guides immunotherapy decisions in lung cancer and other contexts.
- *Prognostic markers*. Ki-67 (a proliferation marker) is used to stratify some cancers.
- *Mismatch repair status*. MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 IHC identifies mismatch repair deficient tumors that may benefit from immunotherapy.

IHC has become essential for modern cancer pathology. Standard diagnostic workups for many cancer types include specific IHC panels.

## What this chapter gives you

Cancer diagnosis is a multi-step process integrating clinical assessment, imaging, biopsy, and pathology. Each step has its own limitations, and the diagnosis emerges from the integration.

Imaging modalities include CT (workhorse for body imaging), MRI (better soft tissue contrast, especially for CNS, soft tissue tumors, breast, and pelvis), ultrasound (real-time, portable, no radiation), and PET (metabolic and molecular imaging, increasingly with specialized tracers like PSMA PET for prostate cancer). Each modality has specific strengths and limitations, and many workups use multiple modalities.

Biopsy techniques range from minimally invasive (fine-needle aspiration) to more substantial (core needle biopsy, excisional biopsy). The choice depends on the lesion location, the planned management, and the need for sufficient tissue for molecular testing. Image-guided percutaneous biopsy is the standard approach for most deep lesions.

Histology — examination of tissue by a pathologist — remains the foundation of cancer diagnosis. H&E staining reveals cell morphology and tissue architecture. Immunohistochemistry adds antibody-based protein staining for lineage identification, differential diagnosis, and therapeutic biomarkers.

The pathology report integrates findings and renders the diagnosis that guides treatment. Additional molecular studies (genetic profiling, biomarker testing) — covered in 18B — increasingly augment the histological diagnosis with information about therapeutic targets and prognostic factors.

Chapter 18B turns to the molecular diagnostics that increasingly drive treatment selection, the staging systems that capture the extent of disease, and the emerging technologies including liquid biopsy that are reshaping how cancers are characterized and monitored.
