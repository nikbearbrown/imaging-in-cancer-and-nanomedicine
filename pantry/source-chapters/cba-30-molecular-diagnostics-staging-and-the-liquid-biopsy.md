Cancer diagnosis used to end at "adenocarcinoma." Now it begins there.

Hold the framing. Twenty years ago, a pathologist reading a lung adenocarcinoma biopsy gave the patient a diagnosis. Today, that same diagnosis is the starting point for a molecular workup: EGFR, ALK, ROS1, BRAF, MET, RET, NTRK, HER2, KRAS, NRG1, PD-L1, tumor mutational burden, microsatellite instability. Each of these tests may identify a specific molecular feature that determines whether a particular targeted therapy will work for that patient. The histological diagnosis tells us the tumor is a lung adenocarcinoma. The molecular workup tells us what to do about it.

The shift from histology-driven to molecularly-driven oncology has been one of the major transformations of the past 25 years. The conceptual framework — *precision medicine* — matches specific therapies to specific tumor features. The infrastructure — next-generation sequencing, multiplex IHC, liquid biopsy assays — supports the framework with practical diagnostic technology. The result is that every newly diagnosed metastatic cancer should ideally undergo comprehensive molecular profiling, and treatment decisions should be informed by that profile.

This chapter — the second on cancer diagnosis — covers the molecular diagnostics that increasingly drive oncology decision-making, the staging systems that capture disease extent and inform prognosis, and the emerging diagnostic technologies including liquid biopsy that are reshaping how cancers are characterized and monitored.

Hold the question: *if every cancer's optimal treatment depends on its molecular profile, what infrastructure is needed to extract that profile reliably and use it intelligently?*

## The molecular diagnostic landscape

Molecular cancer diagnostics has evolved from single-gene tests to comprehensive profiling. The current landscape includes:

*Single-gene tests*. Tests for specific mutations or alterations in specific genes. Examples: EGFR exon 19 deletion testing in lung cancer, BRAF V600E mutation testing in melanoma and other cancers, BRCA1/2 testing for hereditary breast and ovarian cancer.

*Targeted gene panels*. Tests covering 5-100 cancer-related genes simultaneously. Examples: panels for hereditary cancer syndromes, panels for lung cancer molecular profiling, panels for hematological malignancies.

*Comprehensive genomic profiling (CGP)*. Tests covering 300+ cancer-related genes plus structural variants (fusions, amplifications, deletions). Examples: FoundationOne CDx (FDA-approved companion diagnostic), Caris Molecular Intelligence, Tempus xT, Guardant360 (blood-based), Strata Select.

*Whole-exome and whole-genome sequencing*. Comprehensive sequencing of the entire exome (protein-coding regions) or genome. More commonly used in research and academic settings; some clinical applications in pediatric cancers and rare tumors.

*RNA sequencing*. Detects gene expression patterns and fusion transcripts that DNA sequencing might miss. Increasingly part of comprehensive profiling.

*Methylation profiling*. DNA methylation patterns are diagnostic for some tumor types (especially brain tumors, where methylation-based classification has become standard).

*Multiplex IHC and multiplex immunofluorescence*. Examining multiple protein markers simultaneously, sometimes with spatial information about tumor architecture and immune cell infiltration.

*Liquid biopsy*. Blood-based detection of circulating tumor DNA, circulating tumor cells, and tumor-derived extracellular vesicles.

The choice of test depends on the clinical context: the tumor type, the disease stage, the planned treatment, the available specimen, the patient's broader clinical situation. For metastatic cancer where targeted therapy is being considered, comprehensive genomic profiling is increasingly the standard of care.

## Companion diagnostics and biomarker-driven therapy

The intersection between molecular diagnostics and oncology therapy is the *companion diagnostic* — a test that determines whether a specific drug is likely to work in a specific patient. The FDA increasingly approves drugs together with their companion diagnostic, making the test essentially required for drug use.

Major biomarker-drug pairs (a partial list):

*HER2 status and HER2-targeted therapy*. IHC, FISH, or ISH for HER2 expression and amplification in breast and gastric cancer. Determines use of trastuzumab, pertuzumab, T-DM1, T-DXd, and other HER2-targeted agents.

*ER and PR status and hormone therapy*. IHC for estrogen and progesterone receptors in breast cancer. Determines use of tamoxifen, aromatase inhibitors, fulvestrant.

*EGFR mutation status and EGFR inhibitors* in lung cancer. Specific EGFR mutations (L858R, exon 19 deletions, T790M, exon 20 insertions, C797S) determine sensitivity and resistance to different EGFR inhibitors.

*ALK and ROS1 rearrangements and corresponding inhibitors* in lung cancer. FISH or NGS detection.

*BRAF V600E mutation and BRAF inhibitors* in melanoma and other cancers.

*KRAS G12C mutation and sotorasib/adagrasib* in non-small-cell lung cancer.

*BRCA1/2 mutations and PARP inhibitors* in breast, ovarian, pancreatic, prostate cancer.

*Microsatellite instability and immune checkpoint inhibitors* (tumor-agnostic — works across cancer types).

*Tumor mutational burden and immune checkpoint inhibitors*. Higher TMB predicts better response.

*PD-L1 expression and immune checkpoint inhibitors*. Specific thresholds (CPS, TPS) depend on the cancer and the drug.

*NTRK fusions and larotrectinib/entrectinib* (tumor-agnostic).

*RET fusions and selpercatinib/pralsetinib*.

*FGFR2/3 alterations and erdafitinib/pemigatinib*.

*IDH1/IDH2 mutations and ivosidenib/enasidenib* in AML and other cancers.

The list continues to grow. As of 2026, more than 30 specific biomarkers determine treatment decisions in routine oncology. The pace of approval is accelerating.

The clinical implication is that *molecular profiling at diagnosis* is now standard care for most metastatic cancers and for many early-stage cancers with potential adjuvant therapy decisions. The profile guides first-line treatment selection. As treatment proceeds and resistance develops, additional profiling at progression may identify resistance mechanisms and guide subsequent therapy selection.

## Comprehensive genomic profiling

CGP tests examine many cancer-relevant genes simultaneously from a tumor specimen. The technology underlying CGP is *next-generation sequencing (NGS)* — high-throughput parallel sequencing that produces millions of short DNA reads, which are then aligned to a reference genome and analyzed for variants.

A typical CGP test:
- Uses formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tumor tissue (the standard pathology specimen).
- Examines DNA from 300-600+ cancer-related genes for point mutations, small indels, copy number changes, and structural variants.
- Often includes RNA sequencing for fusion detection.
- Reports actionable findings — variants associated with specific therapies (approved or investigational) or specific clinical implications.
- Reports tumor mutational burden, microsatellite instability status, and (sometimes) homologous recombination deficiency status.
- Includes some interpretation — categorizing variants as Tier I (FDA-approved use), Tier II (off-label evidence), Tier III (research evidence), Tier IV (not actionable).

CGP has become standard of care in many settings. Major commercial tests include FoundationOne CDx (the first FDA-approved CGP), Caris Molecular Intelligence, Tempus xT, NeoGenomics InVisionFirst-Lung, and others. Academic medical centers often have internal molecular profiling capabilities.

The interpretation of CGP results requires expertise. Not every variant is actionable. The clinical significance depends on the cancer type, the level of evidence, the patient's prior treatments, and the availability of trials and approved drugs. Many institutions have molecular tumor boards that review CGP results and recommend treatment plans.

Some major findings from large-scale CGP studies:
- *Actionable findings are common*. About 30-50 percent of advanced cancers harbor an alteration with an FDA-approved targeted therapy. Including off-label and clinical trial options brings the actionability rate to 60-80 percent for some cancer types.
- *Tumor type matters less than expected*. Some alterations are tumor-agnostic targets (NTRK fusions, microsatellite instability, RET fusions, BRAF V600E in some contexts). The same drug may work across many tumor types if the molecular feature is present.
- *Variants of unknown significance (VUS)* are common in any individual report. Most VUS turn out to be passengers; some have unrecognized clinical importance. The interpretation evolves over time as more data accumulates.

The challenge of CGP is *implementation*. The technology exists; access varies by institution and country; insurance coverage for the tests has been a barrier; specialist oncology workforce to interpret results is limited. Improving the global access to molecular profiling is one of the major equity issues in modern oncology.

## Cancer staging

Once cancer is diagnosed, *staging* describes the extent of disease. Staging is essential for prognosis, treatment selection, clinical trial enrollment, and standardized communication.

The dominant staging system is the *TNM system* (Tumor, Node, Metastasis), maintained by the American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC) and the International Union Against Cancer (UICC).

*T* describes the primary tumor. Subscripts range from T0 (no evidence of primary tumor) through Tis (in situ), T1-T4 (increasing size or local invasion). The specifics vary by cancer type.

*N* describes regional lymph nodes. N0 (no nodal involvement), N1, N2, N3 (increasing nodal involvement). The specifics vary.

*M* describes distant metastasis. M0 (no distant metastasis), M1 (distant metastasis present). Some systems subdivide M1 (M1a, M1b) based on the metastatic site.

The TNM categories combine into stage groupings (Stage 0, I, II, III, IV), which simplify communication and broadly indicate prognosis. The combinations are cancer-specific — a particular T, N, M combination produces different stage groupings for different cancers.

The AJCC staging manual has been revised multiple times (currently in its 8th edition, published 2017, with planning for the 9th edition underway). Each revision incorporates new data, new molecular markers, and refined understanding of prognostic factors.

Recent staging revisions have incorporated *non-anatomical factors*:
- *Lung cancer*. Tumor size cutoffs refined; staging revised based on outcomes data.
- *Breast cancer*. Hormone receptor status, HER2 status, and Oncotype DX recurrence score are now formally incorporated into staging (the *prognostic stage groups*) for greater accuracy than anatomic stage alone.
- *Prostate cancer*. PSA and Gleason score have been incorporated.
- *Melanoma*. Mitotic rate and ulceration incorporated; revised for thin melanomas.

The integration of molecular biomarkers into staging reflects the recognition that anatomic extent alone doesn't fully capture prognosis. The trend will likely continue, with more cancers incorporating molecular features into formal staging.

*Pathological staging* uses information from surgical specimens after resection. *Clinical staging* uses information available before surgery (history, examination, imaging, biopsy). The two may differ — pathological staging often upstages compared to clinical staging because additional information from surgical specimens reveals more disease than was apparent before.

## Tumor grading

Beyond stage (extent of disease), *grade* (differentiation of tumor cells) is part of cancer characterization. Grade reflects how much the tumor cells resemble their normal tissue counterparts.

- *Grade 1 (well-differentiated)*. Tumor cells resemble normal tissue.
- *Grade 2 (moderately differentiated)*. Intermediate appearance.
- *Grade 3 (poorly differentiated)*. Tumor cells barely resemble their tissue of origin.
- *Grade 4 (undifferentiated)*. Tumor cells have lost recognizable tissue features.

Higher-grade tumors generally have worse prognosis and behave more aggressively. The grading system varies by cancer type. For prostate cancer, the *Gleason score* (sum of the two most common architectural patterns, each scored 3-5; the lowest possible score is now 6, with grade groups 1-5 used in modern reporting). For breast cancer, the *Nottingham grade* (combining tubule formation, nuclear pleomorphism, and mitotic count). For brain tumors, the WHO grades 1-4.

Tumor grade is determined by the pathologist on H&E examination and is part of the standard pathology report. It is increasingly augmented by molecular features that may correlate with or refine the histological grade.

## Liquid biopsy: blood-based cancer diagnostics

Liquid biopsy — using blood, urine, or other body fluids to detect and characterize cancer — has been one of the major developments of the past decade. The technology has multiple applications.

*Circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA)*. Cancer cells release DNA fragments into the bloodstream. The released DNA carries the same mutations as the source cancer cells. Detecting and quantifying tumor-specific mutations in plasma provides a "liquid biopsy" alternative to tissue.

The ctDNA approach has several advantages:
- *Comprehensive sampling*. The DNA in blood represents cells from across the entire tumor and from metastatic sites. Spatial heterogeneity is averaged out.
- *Repeatable*. Blood draws are easily repeatable for monitoring.
- *Accessible when tissue is unavailable*. Some cancers cannot be safely biopsied (some pancreatic tumors, some brain metastases) or insufficient tissue is obtained.
- *Real-time*. ctDNA changes rapidly during therapy, providing early indication of response or resistance.

The applications include:

*Genomic profiling at diagnosis*. When tissue is insufficient for full profiling, ctDNA can provide molecular profile. Multiple FDA-approved liquid biopsy assays for this use (Guardant360 CDx, FoundationOne Liquid CDx, others).

*Resistance monitoring*. Detecting emergence of resistance mutations during targeted therapy. T790M emergence in EGFR-mutant lung cancer triggers switch to osimertinib. ESR1 mutations in metastatic breast cancer indicate hormone therapy resistance and may guide treatment selection.

*Minimal residual disease (MRD) detection*. After curative-intent surgery or therapy, ctDNA can detect microscopic residual disease that imaging cannot see. MRD-positive patients are at high risk of recurrence and may benefit from adjuvant therapy. MRD-negative patients may not need adjuvant therapy. The framework is reshaping adjuvant therapy decisions in colorectal cancer (with the DYNAMIC trial showing reduced chemotherapy with MRD-guided approach) and is expanding to other cancers.

*Treatment response monitoring*. ctDNA levels drop with effective treatment and rise with resistance, often before imaging changes. Serial ctDNA measurements provide real-time response monitoring.

*Multi-cancer early detection*. The newest application — using ctDNA from asymptomatic individuals to detect cancers before symptoms (Chapter 17B). Galleri and similar tests use methylation patterns and other features to detect and identify cancers.

Limitations of ctDNA:
- *Low shedding*. Some tumors release minimal ctDNA, particularly small early-stage cancers and brain tumors (blood-brain barrier limits release). Detection sensitivity is limited for these.
- *Clonal hematopoiesis*. Some mutations in plasma DNA come from age-related expansion of mutated blood cell clones rather than from cancer. Interpretation requires distinguishing cancer mutations from clonal hematopoiesis.
- *Tumor purity in plasma*. The fraction of plasma DNA from tumor varies; at very low fractions, sensitivity for specific mutations is limited.
- *Limited information about tumor structure*. Liquid biopsy provides molecular information but not the spatial or architectural information that tissue pathology provides.

*Circulating tumor cells (CTCs)*. As described in Chapter 13B, intact cancer cells can be detected and counted in blood. CTC enumeration has prognostic value in some cancers and is FDA-cleared for breast, prostate, and colorectal cancers. Molecular characterization of individual CTCs is increasingly possible.

*Tumor-derived extracellular vesicles*. Exosomes and other extracellular vesicles carry tumor-derived proteins and nucleic acids. Some commercial tests detect specific exosomal markers. Applications are emerging.

*Urine-based cancer detection*. ctDNA from urinary tract cancers (bladder, prostate, kidney) can be detected in urine. Several urinary tests are FDA-approved for bladder cancer monitoring.

## The molecular tumor board

Given the complexity of molecular oncology, many cancer centers have established *molecular tumor boards* — multidisciplinary panels that review molecular profiling results and make treatment recommendations.

A typical molecular tumor board includes oncologists (medical, surgical, radiation, pediatric), pathologists, molecular biologists, genetic counselors, clinical trial coordinators, bioinformaticians, and pharmacists. The board reviews each case's molecular profile, identifies actionable findings, considers FDA-approved therapies, considers off-label options, considers clinical trials, considers companion diagnostic implications, and recommends a treatment plan.

The molecular tumor board concept has emerged because:
- *The volume of information is overwhelming*. A typical CGP report contains 50+ variants. Distinguishing which are clinically meaningful requires expertise.
- *The therapeutic landscape changes rapidly*. New drug approvals and trial openings make the optimal decision a moving target.
- *Off-label use requires careful consideration*. Many drugs are used off-label based on molecular features. The evidence base varies.
- *Clinical trial enrollment requires matching*. Matching patients to appropriate molecularly-defined trials requires understanding both the patient's molecular profile and the trial landscape.

The molecular tumor board is one of the institutional adaptations that precision oncology has required. The infrastructure is more developed at major academic centers and less developed in community settings. Reducing this gap is one of the challenges of broader precision oncology implementation.

## Future directions in cancer diagnostics

Several trends are reshaping the field:

*Spatial molecular profiling*. Examining gene expression and protein abundance with spatial resolution within tissue sections. Spatial transcriptomics (Visium, MERFISH, others) and spatial proteomics technologies are providing detailed maps of tumor architecture and microenvironment that bulk profiling cannot match. The clinical applications are emerging.

*Single-cell sequencing*. Profiling individual cells from tumors to characterize cellular heterogeneity. The technology is more established in research than in routine clinical care, but applications in immunotherapy response prediction and minimal residual disease detection are advancing.

*AI in pathology*. Machine learning algorithms for digital pathology image analysis. Several FDA-approved AI tools assist pathologists in specific diagnostic tasks (prostate biopsy interpretation, cancer detection in colorectal biopsies). The technology is rapidly developing.

*Integrated diagnostics*. Combining imaging, pathology, molecular profiling, and clinical data into integrated risk and treatment models. Multiple companies and academic groups are developing such tools.

*Multi-cancer early detection*. As discussed, blood-based screening for multiple cancers simultaneously. Major prospective trials underway.

*Theranostics*. Combining diagnostic imaging with therapy using the same molecular target. PSMA imaging (PET) plus PSMA-targeted radioligand therapy (lutetium-177-PSMA-617, approved 2022) is the leading example. Similar approaches are being developed for somatostatin receptor-expressing tumors (DOTATATE imaging plus lutetium-177-DOTATATE therapy, already in clinical use) and other targets.

The field is moving toward increasingly comprehensive, increasingly multi-modal, increasingly individualized cancer characterization. The infrastructure to support this — both technical and human — is being built in parallel with the technology.

## What this chapter gives you

Molecular diagnostics has transformed cancer characterization. Comprehensive genomic profiling (CGP) examining 300+ cancer-related genes is standard care for most metastatic cancers. The profiling identifies actionable mutations, fusions, amplifications, and other features that determine treatment selection. The list of biomarker-drug pairs is large and growing.

Companion diagnostics — tests that determine whether a specific drug will work in a specific patient — are increasingly integrated into FDA approval pathways. Major examples include HER2 testing for HER2-targeted therapy, EGFR mutation testing for EGFR inhibitors, BRCA1/2 testing for PARP inhibitors, microsatellite instability testing for immunotherapy, and many others.

Staging systems (TNM) capture disease extent and remain essential for prognosis and treatment selection. Modern staging increasingly incorporates molecular biomarkers (HER2, hormone receptors, Oncotype DX in breast cancer; PSA and Gleason score in prostate cancer; mitotic rate in melanoma). The trend toward biology-informed staging will continue.

Liquid biopsy — using blood-based ctDNA, circulating tumor cells, and extracellular vesicles — provides a less invasive complement (and sometimes alternative) to tissue diagnostics. Applications include genomic profiling, resistance monitoring, MRD detection, treatment response monitoring, and multi-cancer early detection. Several FDA-approved liquid biopsy assays are in clinical use, and the technology continues to develop rapidly.

Molecular tumor boards have emerged as the institutional adaptation to molecular complexity, bringing together multidisciplinary expertise to interpret profiling results and recommend treatments.

Future directions include spatial molecular profiling, single-cell sequencing, AI-assisted pathology, integrated diagnostics combining multiple data types, multi-cancer early detection through ctDNA, and theranostics combining diagnosis and therapy using the same molecular target.
